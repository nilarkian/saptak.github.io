    ASPI

    Australian Strategic Policy Institute
    aspi.org.au ↗ Tech_Tracker ↗
    🏛 Canberra, Australia 🏷 Think Tank · Policy · Tech Intelligence
    Government-funded. Editorially independent. Tracks which countries are winning the race for 64 technologies that will define the next 30 years — and publishes the data openly. That combination is rarer than it should be.

    What Is ASPI

    ASPI is Australia's independent, non-partisan think tank on defence and strategic policy. Government-funded but editorially independent — a distinction that matters when the output includes research that names China's technology dominance across 37 of 44 critical technology categories. It publishes reports, runs the Critical Technology Tracker, and operates parallel projects like the Xinjiang Data Project, which maps detention infrastructure in Xinjiang using satellite imagery and open-source data.

    The institutional model — public funding, independent editorial line, open data — is the interesting part. Most government-adjacent research is either captured or classified. ASPI publishes everything.

    Why "Critical Technologies"

    Technologies with the capacity to significantly enhance, or pose risk to, a country's national interests, including economic prosperity, social cohesion, and national security. — Australian Government, 2022

    The term has gone mainstream across Five Eyes governments, the EU, and multilateral bodies. The underlying logic is dual-use: the same technology drives economic growth and enables military advantage, often from the same research pipeline. You can't cleanly separate civilian AI from autonomous weapons development. That's the policy problem.

    Why It Matters Now

    Critical technologies are the primary arena of US–China strategic competition. Three pressure points dominate the policy conversation. First, research concentration — a handful of countries lead output across most domains, and single-country monopoly risk now registers as a security concern, not just an economic one. Next, supply chain dependencies — access to critical minerals, semiconductors, and biotech inputs gets treated as a security issue, not a procurement issue. Finally, the dual-use dilemma — civilian and military applications can't be separated in AI, quantum, or biotech, which makes export controls complicated and intelligence harder to read.

    Cross-Cutting Enablers

    Two domains function as force multipliers across all others — less individual technologies, more infrastructure that makes every other domain faster and harder to secure.

    Quantum
    Quantum sensors sharpen navigation, radar, and imaging. Post-quantum cryptography is a prerequisite for securing every other domain — once it breaks, everything built on current encryption breaks with it.
    AI / ML
    Accelerates drug discovery, materials science, and autonomous systems simultaneously. Less a domain than a layer that sits under everything else and makes it faster.

    Why It's In Here
    Open data, government-independent, geopolitically honest. The Critical Technology Tracker answers "who's actually winning" with citations, not opinions — and the answer is often uncomfortable for Western audiences. That's exactly the kind of source worth keeping close.
